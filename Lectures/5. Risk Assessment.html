5. Risk Assessment
  • Source: NIST SP 800-30r1, Guide for Conducting Risk Assessments

Risk Management Processes

Risk Framing

The very first step in risk management is to "frame" risk. Framing refers to defining a context for evaluating risks and describing the environment in which risk-based decisions are made. The purpose of the risk framing component is to produce a risk management strategy that addresses how organizations intend to assess risk, respond to risk, and monitor risk.
Framing is influenced by an organization's mission, values, priorities, strategic directions, or even individual styles of management or thought processes. Therefore it is extremely important to establish framing as the first step.

  • Determine what your (or your organization's) priorities are (based on the organizational mission). What are your risk is framed as:
    • Financials (money), or market evaluation (stock market)
    • Regulatory Capture (laws, regulations, market regulations)
    • Risk to systems, public safety, or national security
    • Risk to reputation, brand, or trademark
    • Market share,
    • etc. ...

Risk Assessment

Risk Assessment is the second process of Risk Management, and is how organizations assess risk within the context of the organizational risk frame. The purpose of Risk Assessment is to inform decision-makers and support risk responses by identifying:

  • Relevant threats to an organization or threats directed through the organization against others
  • Vulnerabilities, both internal and external
  • Impact (level of harm)
  • Likelihood (probability of occurrence)

The result is the determination of risk, which is usually a function of likelihood and impact.
NIST SP 800-30r1 provides a guideline on how to conduct a risk assessment. It includes fundamental information about the risk management process and describes the process for assessing information security risks.

Risk Response

The third component of risk management addresses how organizations respond to risk once that risk is determined based on the results of a risk assessment. The purpose of the risk response component is to provide a consistent, organization-wide response to risk in accordance with the organizational risk frame by:

  • developing alternative courses of action for responding to risk
  • evaluating the alternative courses of action;
  • determining appropriate courses of action consistent with organizational risk tolerance;
  • implementing risk responses based on selected courses of action.

Risk Monitoring

Once the risks have been assessed, and appropriate measures were taken to respond to risks, the risks need to be monitored and reevaluated in order to:

  • determine the ongoing effectiveness of risk responses (consistent with the organizational risk frame)
  • identify risk-impacting changes to organizational information systems and the environments in which the systems operate
  • verify that planned risk responses are implemented and information security requirements and guidelines are satisfied

Fundamentals of Risk Assessment

Risk assessments are NOT universal. Risks and risk assessments are often very specific to individuals and organizations and depend on factors such as environment, missions, goals, priorities, and other factors. Combinations of these factors differentiate individuals and organizations, so please beware that there is no "standard risk assessment" that you can find for any specific system.

Very Important Note:

Please do not mistake or misuse industry numbering systems or measurement methods such as CVEs, and CVSS scores with risk. Risk is calculated individually for each system as deployed and used in its specific environment. For example, a CVSS Score of 10.0 (the highest possible CVSS score) for a vulnerability, has absolutely nothing to do with risk to a system, and risk can be 0 or non-existent (eliminated) due to a particular situation of a system, while a vulnerability with CVSS of 1.0 might pose significant risk to a system. Publications and media often make big deal out of the numbers of vulnerability enumerations (CVEs) and scores (CVSS), but these are meaningless in the context of Risk!

Purpose of Risk Assessment

The results of risk assessments are used to make strategic and tactical decisions to address the potential adverse impacts on organization operations, assets, and Interests.

Some examples of practical use cases for risk assessments are:

  • to assist in the design and implementation of information systems
  • To inform/influence managerial decision-making:
    • To grant or deny authorization to implement/operate information systems
    • Decisions on modification of missions/business functions and/or mission/business processes based on trends or risks
    • To direct investment or spending
  • to communicate complex (and potentially highly technical) issues with stakeholders (when they do not have the technical background to understand or evaluate technical details)
  • etc. ...

Risk Assessment Methodology

Risk assessment methodologies are usually developed and defined during the risk framing step and are kept consistent for the organization at large (all risk assessments use the same methodology). Although multiple methodologies can be employed depending on specific needs. If multiple methodologies are employed, the results using different methodologies are not comparable.

An explicit risk model and methodology demands a consistent assessment approach, analysis, and rationale for assessed values and increases repeatability and reproducibility of risk assessments.

Risk assessment methodology typically includes:

  • Risk Assessment Process
  • Explicit Risk Model
    • Definitions and key terms are defined
    • Risk factors are defined
    • Risk is defined: Risk=...
  • Assessment Approach
    • How to assign values to risk factors (qualitative, quantitative, semi-qualitative)
  • Analysis Approach
    • How combinations of risk factors are identified/analyzed to ensure adequate coverage of the problem space at a consistent level of detail (in other words, comparing apples-to-apples)

Risk Model

Risk is a calculated value. The elements used to define risk are called risk factors. Risk is a calculation of the relationship between risk factors. Different risk models and formulas exist which are defined by various organizations for various purposes and industries that may include industry-specific formulas and risk factors. Typical risk factors are threat, vulnerability, impact, likelihood, and predisposing conditions. You should be already familiar with these terms and their NIST definitions from previous material.

Examples of Risk Model:

  • From Mozilla Foundation "Assessing Risk": R = likelihood * Impact
  • TRA-1 (The Harmonized Threat and Risk Assessment Methodology,, Government of Canada): R = ƒ (AVal , T, V) where AVal=asset value, T=threat and V=Vulnerabilities

Please study NIST SP 800-30r1, Guide for Conducting Risk Assessments chapter 2 for an explanation of concepts such as:

  • Threat / Threat source (and types)
  • Threat event
  • Threat shifting
  • TTP (tactics, techniques, and procedures)
  • Vulnerabilities (and where vulnerabilities are found)
  • Predisposing Conditions
  • threat scenarios
    • risk materializes as a result of a series of threat events
    • Threat-event are materialized when threats succeed in exploiting vulnerabilities or due to predisposing conditions
  • Likelihood
    • intent, capability, targeting
    • historical evidence, Imperial data
    • assessed based on a specific time frame
    • estimated frequency of the event
    • Can also have factors such as the likelihood of occurrence, the likelihood of adverse impact
  • Impact
  • Risk
    • Risk aggregation
    • Temporal Risk (Risk factors are associated with a specific time period)
  • Uncertainty

Assessment and Analysis Approaches

  • Quantitative assessments: Use of numbers (e.g. 1, 5, 1000, 256, ...)
  • Qualitative assessments: Use of non-numerical categories or levels (high, low, severe, ...)
  • Semi-quantitative assessments: Uses bins (0-10, 10-20, 20-30, ...), scales (1 out of 20), or representative numbers (1-10)
    The analytic approach can vary depending on the risk framing:
  • Threat oriented
  • asset/impact oriented
  • vulnerability oriented
  • Rigorous analysis approaches such as mathematical modelling and graph-based analyses

Risk Assessment Process

Key activities and guidelines are provided in Chapter 3 of NIST SP 800-30r1, Guide for Conducting Risk Assessments.
Additional resources are provided in the appendix of the same document as well. These include the tabulated list of typical items for each risk factor (Appendix D to H), as well as guidelines on how to write a risk assessment report (Appendix K).

  • Step 1: Prepare
    • Purpose
    • Scope
    • Assumptions and constraints
    • Sources of information
    • Risk model and analytic approaches
  • Step 2: Conduct
    • Threat sources + Threat Events
    • Vulnerabilities
    • Likelihood
    • Impact
    • Risk
  • Step 3: Communicate (results, Reports)
  • Step 4: maintain (re-assess)
