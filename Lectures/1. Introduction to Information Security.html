1. Introduction to Information Security

What is Information Security

The practice of protecting information by:

  • Identifying and mitigating risks
  • detecting and responding to incidents

It is typically achieved through structured Information Security Management.

  • Formal Frameworks provide recommendations and guidelines

The principle concept behind information security is the protection and maintenance of CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability) of information.

Information Security Management

Consists of:

  • Identifying all assets
  • Evaluation of risks to the assets (Risk assessment)
  • Designing or selecting appropriate measures and controls (Risk treatment)
    • Eliminate, mitigate, outsource, or accept the risks (Risk management)
  • Monitoring and detection of incidents
  • Response to incidents (Incident management)

Information Security vs. Cyber/IT Security

  • Information Security encompasses all forms of information in any medium
  • Information Security also covers all assets of an organization or business
    • Hardware and software
    • Documentation and knowledge
    • People (employees, suppliers, customers, partners, …)
  • Cybersecurity is a subset of the larger information security

What is “Information”?

  • ISO definition: facts, concepts, or instructions
  • NIST Definition: “Facts or ideas, which can be represented (encoded) as various forms of data; (2) Knowledge (e.g., data, instructions) in any medium or form that can be communicated between system entities.” (read NIST SP 800-12r1, Section 1.4 for definitions)

Information Security Frameworks

  • Set of practices, policies and processes to provide a calculated or standard approach to risk management
    • provide strategies for response management
  • Help organizations to develop repeatable and measurable processes
  • To provide security practitioners with a common language, common measures, and benchmarks in comparison to peer organizations

Examples:

  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework (https://www.nist.gov/cyberframework)
  • ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management) (https://www.iso.org/isoiec-27001-information-security.html)
  • PCI (Payment Card Industry) (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/pci_security/standards_overview)

Reasons for Adopting Frameworks

  • To genuinely get what a framework provides:
    • To better accomplish the protection of sensitive information (organizations’ own, or other’s information)
    • Use sets of tried and true best practices and recommendations
    • Use of common language, measures, and benchmarks
  • Regulatory and legal compliance (Example: GDPR, HIPPA, PCI, PIPEDA, etc.)
  • Public safety or national welfare (governments and government contractors)
  • Product or business differentiation
  • Reputation and financial security

Some Relevant Information Security Laws & Regulations

How do these apply to me as an IT pro (why do I care)?

  • know and comply with local and international laws
    • The Internet is international!
    • Always verify local and industry-specific regulatory requirements
  • The organization you work for may have compliance requirements
    • Affects your duties, responsibilities, and day-to-day activities as an employee, directly or indirectly
  • General knowledge of Information Security Frameworks provides you:
    • Opportunities to do a much better job and demonstrate competencies
    • Opportunities to have intelligent and informed conversations with the interested parties
  • You may be asked to obtain certifications, or have general knowledge of specific frameworks
    • Human resource development is part of (at least NIST and ISO) frameworks, so chances are your organization may provide or pay for required training for your role

PIPEDA (Canada)

  • The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act
    • “applies to private-sector organizations across Canada that collect, use, or disclose personal information in the course of a commercial activity.”
    • “10 fair information principles”
  • See: PIPEDA requirements in brief
  • Enforced by Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC)

CASL (Canada)

  • Canadian Anti-Spam Law
    • “protects consumers and businesses from the misuse of digital technology, including spam and other electronic threats.”
  • Enforced by:
    • Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC)
    • Canadian Radio-Television and Telecommunications Commissions (CRTC)
    • Federal Competition Bureau
      see: https://fightspam.gc.ca

PHIPA (Ontario)

  • Personal Health Information Protection Act, 2004
    • To “establish rules for the collection, use and disclosure of personal health information …”
    • To “provide individuals with a right of access to personal health information …”
    • “provide individuals with a right to require the correction or amendment …”
    • To “provide for independent review and resolution of complaints …”
  • See Personal Health Information Protection Act, 2004, S.O. 2004, c. 3, Sched. A
  • Governs the manner "personal health information" is collected, used, and disclosed within the "health sector"
    • Regulates "custodian" (as defined in the act) and any individual or business that receive information from custodians
    • Does not apply to information collected directory from individuals by any entity that is not a healthcare provider
      • For example, the information your insurance company collects from you is not protected under PHIPA, but if they collect information from your doctor or any healthcare professional, then it would be covered by PHIPA

FIPPA (Ontario)

  • Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act, 1990
    • “… to protect the privacy of individuals with respect to personal information about themselves held by institutions and to provide individuals with a right of access to that information”
  • See: Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.31

HIPAA (USA)

  • https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/index.html
  • Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996
  • USA federal law
    • Both a regulation and a framework
    • HIPAA Security Rule compliance
  • Primarily used in the USA
  • Goal: Confidentiality, integrity, and availability of electronic protected health information (eHIP)

The Patriot Act (USA)

  • US Congress “Uniting and Strengthening America by Providing Appropriate Tools Required to Intercept and Obstruct Terrorism (USA PATRIOT) Act of 2001” in response to 9/11 attacks
    • https://www.congress.gov/107/plaws/publ56/PLAW-107publ56.pdf
  • Many information and security implications, For example:
    • Expanded surveillance abilities of law enforcement, including by tapping domestic and international phones;
    • Make it a requirement to include the name of the originator in wire transfer instructions sent to the United States and other countries, with the information to remain with the transfer from its origination until the point of disbursement (heavy penalties for non-complying institutions)
    • Permission given to law enforcement to search property and records without a warrant, consent, or knowledge

Examples of impacts to non-US entities

  • Individuals, Organizations, and businesses outside the USA may be compelled to comply!
  • If you and your organization are in possession of legally protected data, you must consider additional risks due to the PATRIOT Act when using any products or services from outside your organization that can be hosted or stored in the USA
    • The USA PATRIOT Act invalidates reasonable expectation of privacy of any information in possession of any US-based or non-US entity doing business in the US.
    • Examples:
      • Protected private data of Canadians (such as health data) stored on servers owned by USA-based cloud providers may not meet requirements of applicable privacy laws and regulations, as they are subject to "secret" searches by the US Government
        • Some provinces such as BC and Nova Scotia have passed additional laws and regulations to mitigate risks to legally protected health information
        • Canadian Government:
          • Privacy Matters (The Federal Strategy to Address Concerns About the USA PATRIOT Act and Transborder Data Flows)
          • Guidance Document: Taking Privacy into Account Before Making Contracting Decisions

More detailed information on Wikipedia as a starting point: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriot_Act

GDPR (EU)

  • General Data Protection Regulation (EU) (https://gdpr.eu/)

The GDPR applies to all companies handling the personal data of EU residents, including companies established outside the EU if they offer goods or services to EU residents or monitor their behaviour. The GDPR entered into force on May 25, 2018.

See: The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation

  • Updated definition of personal data: Location data and online identifiers are now expressly included in the definition of personal data.
  • Comprehensive record-keeping obligation: Records are used to demonstrate compliance.
  • Stricter definition of consent: Specific, informed and unambiguous consent must be freely given by a statement or by clear affirmative action. Individuals can withdraw their consent at any time.
  • New rights for individuals: Individuals have the right to access, transfer, correct and restrict their personal data and to ask that it be destroyed.
  • Requirement to appoint a data protection officer: Companies processing personal data on a large scale must appoint a data protection officer.
  • Data breach notification requirement: Competent supervisory authorities within the EU must generally be informed within 72 hours of a personal data breach.
  • Diversified toolkit of mechanisms: The toolkit is provided to lawfully transfer data outside the EU; the transfer is subject to specific conditions and safeguards.
  • Penalties for non-compliance: Companies would be subject to fines of up to 4% of global annual turnover or €20 million, whichever is higher.

Canadian Perspective:

  • "In 2001, the EU recognized Canada's Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) as providing adequate protection."
    • if you meet PIPEDA requirements, you may already meet GDPR requirements
  • "Canada's adequacy status ensures that data processed in accordance with the GDPR can be subsequently transferred from the EU to Canada without requiring additional data protection safeguards (for example, standard contractual rules) or authorization to transfer the data."
