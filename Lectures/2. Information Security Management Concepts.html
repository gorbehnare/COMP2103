2. Information Security Management Concepts

Definitions and terminology

Please note that different standards may use the same terms, however, the meaning and the definitions of the terms may vary. Unless otherwise specified, we are using the NIST definitions for all terminology.

Policy

  • “statements, rules or assertions that specify the correct or expected behaviour of an entity.”
  • Policy may also refer to “specific rules” or “specific managerial decisions”, or may define the use of engagement or behaviour
  • Examples: Policy against use of abusive language, policy on gifts and entertainment

Standard

  • “uniform use of specific technologies, parameters, or procedures when such uniform use will benefit an organization”
  • Standards are broad and generic, so they can be applied to as many things as possible

Guide or Guideline

  • Assist in meeting standards for a less broad area of interest
    • For example, a guideline can assist in developing “system-specific procedures”
  • To ensure that specific security measures required by standards are not overlooked

Procedure

  • Describe how to implement a system to meet applicable security policies and standards
  • May follow guidelines to ensure compliance
  • Can and may contain detailed steps to be followed by users, system operations personnel, or others to accomplish a particular task or implementation of a system

Information Security Policy

  • An “aggregate of directives, regulations, rules, and practices that prescribes how an organization manages, protects, and distributes information”
  • It is specific to an organization

Program Policy

  • Used to create an organization’s information security program
  • Components: Purpose, Scope, Responsibilities, Compliance

Issue-Specific Policy

  • Specific policies that focus on narrower systems or use of systems or technology
  • Examples:
    • issue-specific policy on smartphones during class
    • Issue-specific policy on the use of corporate email system

System-specific policy

  • Example: Policies on employee access to specific documents, Policies on email security (such as filtering and scanning requirements)

Roles and Responsibilities

Information security frameworks require that roles and responsibilities be clearly defined and communicated.

As an example, PIPEDA (Canada privacy law) under "Fair Information Principle 1 – Accountability" requires organizations to "... appoint someone to be responsible for your organization’s PIPEDA compliance." and include contact information for individuals to be able to communicate with your organization regarding privacy. For more information see PIPEDA Requirements in brief and PIPEDA Fair Information Principle 1 – Accountability

Everyone is involved in information security. However, an organization or business must clearly define who is responsible for:

  • Defining an approach to addressing risk across the entire organization
  • Developing organizational risk management strategy
  • Supporting information-sharing amongst authorizing officials and other senior leaders within the organization
  • Overseeing risk management-related activities across the organization

Some job titles, roles responsibilities and definitions are provided by NIST. It is recommended that you read the list provided in SP 800-12r1 (An Introduction to Information Security), Chapter 3 "Roles and Responsibilities" to be familiar with and understand the definitions of such roles and their defined responsibilities. Some of these roles include:

  • CEO
  • CIO
  • Information Owner/steward
  • SAISO / CISO
  • AO and AO designated representatives
  • SAOP
  • Common Control Provider
  • System Owner
  • SSO
  • Information Security Architect
  • SSE
  • Security Control Assessor
  • System Administrator
  • User

More Information Security Definitions

The CIA

The goal of information security in general is to protect the "CIA triad" (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability).

  • Confidentiality:
    • "Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure, including means for protecting personal privacy and proprietary information"
    • "The property that sensitive information is not disclosed to unauthorized entities"
    • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/confidentiality
  • Integrity:
    • "guarding against improper information modification or destruction, and includes ensuring information non-repudiation and authenticity"
    • "A property whereby data has not been altered in an unauthorized manner since it was created, transmitted or stored."
    • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/integrity
  • Availability:
    • "Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information"
    • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/availability

Threat Source

Threat Sources are sometimes referred to as "Threat Actors" or "Threat Agents" as well. This may be defined as:

  • "a malicious person with harmful intent or an unintended or unavoidable situation (such as a natural disaster, technical failure, or human error) that may trigger a vulnerability."
  • "The intent and method targeted at the intentional exploitation of a vulnerability or a situation and method that may accidentally trigger a vulnerability. Synonymous with Threat Agent."
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/threat_source

There are two general categories of Threat Sources:

  • Adversarial Threats:

    • Individuals, groups, organizations, agents or entities that are actively seeking to threaten confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information system

  • Non-adversarial Threats:

    • Non-actors or actors that may threaten your system due to errors and omissions, or natural disasters

  • Examples of Threat-source (who)

    • insider (for example employee), outsider (for example a visitor, or an internet user), malicious hackers, system administrator, technical failures

Threat

  • "An activity, deliberate or unintentional, with the potential for causing harm to an automated information system or activity."
  • A possible danger to a computer system, which may result in the interception, alteration, obstruction, or destruction of computational resources, or other disruption to the system.
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/threat

Threat Event

  • "An event or situation that has the potential for causing undesirable consequences or impact."
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/threat_event

Vulnerability and Predisposing Condition

  • Vulnerability:
    • "Weakness in an information system, system security procedures, internal controls, or implementation that could be exploited or triggered by a threat source."
    • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/vulnerability
  • Vulnerabilities are not just "technical". Examples of non-technical vulnerabilities:
    • Relationships, policy changes, laws, regulations, decision-making, external parties and suppliers, etc.
  • predisposing condition:
    • "A condition that exists within an organization, a mission/business process, enterprise architecture, or information system including its environment of operation, which contributes to (i.e., increases or decreases) the likelihood that one or more threat events, once initiated, will result in undesirable consequences or adverse impact to organizational operations and assets, individuals, other organizations, or the Nation."
    • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/predisposing_condition

Risk

Risk is represented as a calculated or measured value.

  • "A measure of the extent to which an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event (threat), and typically a function of: (i) the adverse impacts that would arise if the circumstance or event occurs; and (ii) the likelihood of occurrence."
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/risk

Control

  • "A measure that is modifying risk. (Note: controls include any process, policy, device, practice, or other actions that modify risk.)"
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/control

Controls can be "Policy-based" or "Technical". Generally, a combination of both, technical and policy-based controls, are implemented in order to balance risk "treatment" and associated costs.

Example: If a computer system is vulnerable to virus infection, some example controls can include the use of antivirus software, restricting access to the system, for example disconnecting from the internet and network, air-gaping, restricting access to storage media, restricting physical access to the system, accounting (log who accessed the system, when, and why, so the party can be held accountable for any breach of policy), etc.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides a standard set of "Control Families" and recommends a set of controls so practitioners and auditors can assess compliance based on standardized terminology. As of this writing NIST SP800-53 Rev.5 specifies 20 categories of "control families" containing 1189 "controls".

Risk Management

  • "Coordinated activities to direct and control an organization with regard to risk."
  • "The process of managing risks to agency operations (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), agency assets, or individuals resulting from the operation of an information system. It includes risk assessment; cost-benefit analysis; the selection, implementation, and assessment of security controls; and the formal authorization to operate the system. The process considers effectiveness, efficiency, and constraints due to laws, directives, policies, or regulations."
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/risk_management

Risk Management is an ongoing process consisting of the following components and activities:

  • Framing Risk: establishing a risk context (how risk is framed? based on money, reputation, production, public relations, etc.)
  • Assessing Risk: a risk assessment process is used to identify and evaluate risks, based on the framing
  • Responding to Risk: The following are possible responses to reprieved risk:
    • Eliminate
    • Mitigate: put controls in place to reduce the likelihood or impact of threat event (lower risk)
    • Outsource: for example purchase insurance
    • Accept: do nothing, accept risk as cost of doing business
  • Monitoring: verifying that the treatments and controls are still valid and taking action if risks are changing over time

It is noteworthy to be aware that in larger organizations where roles and responsibilities are delegated, the higher tiers or organization are often dealing with "Strategic Risks", while the lower tiers and front-line workers may be dealing with "Tactical Risks". Having awareness of such differences in perspective may be helpful in your future endeavours!
