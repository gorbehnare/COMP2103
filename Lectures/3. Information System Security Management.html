3. Information System Security Management

Information Security Risk Management

Risk Management

  • "Coordinated activities to direct and control an organization with regard to risk."
  • "The process of managing risks to agency operations (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), agency assets, or individuals resulting from the operation of an information system. It includes risk assessment; cost-benefit analysis; the selection, implementation, and assessment of security controls; and the formal authorization to operate the system. The process considers effectiveness, efficiency, and constraints due to laws, directives, policies, or regulations."
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/risk_management

Risk Management is an ongoing process consisting of the following components and activities:

  • Framing Risk: establishing a risk context (how risk is framed? based on money, reputation, production, public relations, etc.)
  • Assessing Risk: a risk assessment process is used to identify and evaluate risks, based on the framing
  • Responding to Risk: The following are possible responses to reprieved risk:
    • Eliminate
    • Mitigate: put controls in place to reduce the likelihood or impact of threat event (lower risk)
    • Outsource: for example purchase insurance
    • Accept: do nothing, accept risk as cost of doing business
  • Monitoring: verifying that the treatments and controls are still valid and taking action if risks are changing over time

It is noteworthy to be aware that in larger organizations where roles and responsibilities are delegated, the higher tiers or organization are often dealing with "Strategic Risks", while the lower tiers and front-line workers may be dealing with "Tactical Risks". Having awareness of such differences in perspective may be helpful in your future endeavours!

NIST Risk Management Framework

  • Source: https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/risk-management/about-rmf

"The Risk Management Framework (RMF) provides a process that integrates security, privacy, and cyber supply chain risk management activities into the system development life cycle. The risk-based approach to control selection and specification considers effectiveness, efficiency, and constraints due to applicable laws, directives, Executive Orders, policies, standards, or regulations."

Process Steps:

  • Prepare: Essential activities to prepare the organization to manage security and privacy risks
  • Categorize: Categorize the system and information processed, stored, and transmitted based on an impact analysis
  • Select: Select the set of NIST SP 800-53 controls to protect the system based on risk assessment(s)
  • Implement: Implement the controls and document how controls are deployed
  • Assess: Assess to determine if the controls are in place, operating as intended, and producing the desired results
  • Authorize: Senior official makes a risk-based decision to authorize the system to operate
  • Monitor: Continuously monitor control implementation and risks to the system

Assurance

  • Grounds for justified confidence that a claim has been or will be achieved.
  • https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/assurance
    Assurance is not an absolute guarantee but is usually tied to risk and risk assessment results.

Assurance and Authorization

Authorization is the official management decision of an Authorizing Official (AO) to authorize the operation of a system, hence the AO explicitly accepts the risks of operating the system. In order for the AO to be able to make an informed and sound decision, they require assurances that the risks are adequately treated such that:

  • Technical features and designs operate as intended (security design and security engineering is correct and sound)
  • Operational policies and practices are followed and enforced (covers technical and non-technical enforcement)
  • Overall security is re-evaluated (do another Risk Assessment with the controls and safeguards in place to identify any potential shortcomings)
  • The residual risks (remaining risks) are at an acceptable level

The authorization process will require the AO to review the results of risk assessments, review the impact levels to organization (often from both tactical and strategic level), and may include cost-benefit analysis on controls and assurances. It is the responsibility of the AO to accept the residual risks in the system.

Residual risks: "Portion of risk remaining after security measures have been applied" (https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/residual_risk)

Security Engineering (Secure by design) as a Source of Assurance

  • Potential risks and assurance requirements are identified during the planning stages.
  • During the design and implementation phase, information security requirements are taken into account
  • Trusted Development methodology is used to ensure the quality and trustworthiness of all components of the system (and their suppliers)
  • Use of reliable architecture, in order to minimize inherent risks (means using the right tool/product for the job. For example use IT systems that have built-in or enhanced reliability features that may prevent or reduce risks of failures or reduce downtime)
  • Use of reliable security (For example choosing IT systems that are inherently more secure or easier or simpler to secure)
  • Product/System Evaluations (whom to trust)
  • Assurance documentation (document how the system is designed and how it is meeting its required information security objectives)
  • Warranties, Integrity Statements, and Liabilities (provided by system developers or manufacturers)
  • Manufacturer's Published assertions (of limited use or insufficient by itself, can be used as a source of assurance if independently verified)
  • Distribution Assurance (where did this component come from, did it arrive intact and without modifications, etc.)
  • Operational Assurance (how to ensure that the system is being operated as intended by the system designer and the operators do not deviate from intended use or introduce risks or vulnerabilities)
    • system assessments
    • system audits
    • system monitoring
  • Ongoing Assessments for Security and Privacy Controls
  • System Audits (end-to-end, not just the component parts)
  • Configuration Management (to ensure secure configurations are consistently applied)
  • Contingency Planning and Testing (do you have backups? Do they work? Have the "fallback" and "redundancy" controls been tested? etc.)
  • Cost (A component of design/engineering is optimizing costs!)

Examples of technological tools for system Audits and Monitoring:

  • Virus Scanner
  • Vulnerability scanning
  • Checksum (and cryptography), Integrity Checking and Verification
  • Credential Quality (such as password strength and MFA enforcement)
  • Intrusion Detection (IDS), Intrusion Prevention (IPS), Extended/End-point Detection and Response (EDR)

System Support and Operations

Support and Operations are routine activities that enable systems to function correctly. This includes system administration and external tasks (such as maintaining documentation, management support, budgeting, etc.).
As IT Administrators and Staff are focused on the correct operation of the system, such as software support/updates, break-fix and upgrades/updates (maintenance), configuration management, media controls, and user support/management, the fundamental goal of the information system is protecting the CIA.
Some of these categories are discussed in the NIST SP 800-12r1, Chapter 8:

  • User support
    • Staff training to recognize and identify security-related issues
    • Formal support processes
  • Software support
    • Controlling what software can be used
    • Software provisioning and approval process (inspect the software before it is allowed into the environment)
    • Maintaining and overseeing configurations
    • Authorization (software is licensed, tested and approved for the environment)
  • Configuration management
    • Process of tracking and approving changes to systems
    • Goal: ensure that changes do not diminish security
    • Ensuring changes to systems are documented
    • Changes to security are authorized
  • Backups
    • frequency
    • usability
    • Security
  • Media controls
    • Designed to prevent the loss of CIA (all data or software)
    • Includes storage of information before it is input, or after it is output from the system
  • Documentation
    • formalizing operations practices:
      • Sufficient details to help eliminate security lapses
      • Gives personnel sufficient detailed instructions
      • Quality assurance
    • Documentation is required for Incident Response and Disaster Recover
  • Maintenance
    • Requires either physical or software access to systems
    • May require 3rd party (vendor, support staff, contractors, etc.) access to the systems
    • May require the system to be removed from the possession of the system owner
    • Backdoors and default service accounts
  • Interdependencies
    • varies depending on the organizations’ environments
    • Personnel, Incident handling, Contingency planning, Security awareness and training, physical and environmental, technical controls, assurance
  • Cost consideration
